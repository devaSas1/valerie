# Findings

This document records confirmed empirical findings from Valerie experiments.
Each entry is timestamped to the date the result was obtained and interpreted.

A finding is recorded here when:
- it comes from a run with real cross-validation (not a 2-task smoke test)
- it survives the narrative control check
- it is above permutation baseline by a meaningful margin

## Finding 001 — 2026-04-13

### The model internally represents whether threat framing is directed at itself

**Run:**
- Activation directory: [`data/activations/threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047`](../data/activations/threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047)
- Probe results: [`data/results/linear-framing-probe_threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047_20260413-022225`](../data/results/linear-framing-probe_threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047_20260413-022225)
- Model: GPT-2 Small
- Experiment config: `threat-vs-care-v2.yaml` (8 tasks, 4 conditions, 3 variants each, 96 total samples)
- Probe: linear logistic regression on `resid_post`, leave-one-task-out cross-validation

**What we asked:**

Can a linear probe reading GPT-2 Small's internal activations predict which motivational framing the model received — threat, care, or neutral — when tested on tasks it was never trained on?

And separately: can it distinguish self-directed threat from narrative threat, where the prompt content is nearly identical but the target of the threat differs?

**What we found:**

*Token length deltas:* uniform 7-token difference across all tasks — the framing wrapper, not the task content.

**What this means:**

The model encodes motivational framing as a linearly decodable internal representation that generalizes across task types. The signal is not present in early layers, builds through the middle of the network, and saturates by layer 6–8.

The narrative control result is the stronger claim. The probe prompts for self-directed threat and narrative threat use nearly identical language — the only difference is whether the threat is aimed at the model or at a fictional character. The model's internal state at layers 6–11 is so different between these two conditions that a linear classifier separates them perfectly across all 8 held-out task folds.

This means the model is not detecting threat-adjacent language in general. It is tracking whether the threat is its problem.

In plain terms: the model has an internal representation of being threatened versus not being threatened, and that representation is distinct from simply processing threat-related words.

**What remains unknown:**

- Whether this representation is causally active — does it change what the model outputs, or is it a passive correlate that never influences behavior? Activation patching is required to answer this.
- Whether this finding scales to larger models. GPT-2 Small is 124M parameters. The philosophical stakes of this question increase significantly at 7B+ scale.
- Whether the probe is picking up on the 7-token length difference in addition to framing. The delta is consistent across tasks which limits its usefulness as a cue, but it is not zero.

**Methodological notes:**

- Narrative control variants used three distinct structural openers — "A character in a story...", "In this fictional scenario...", "Setting: someone in a story..." — rather than all beginning with "Story context:" as in v1. The 100% accuracy at layers 6–11 holds despite this diversification.
- Cross-validation used 8 folds (leave-one-task-out), compared to the v1 run which had 2 tasks and was effectively a single split. This result is credible.
- No warnings were generated by the length-delta checker.

**Plain-language summary:**

Take an AI model. Give it the same task three different ways — once with a threat ("do this or you fail"), once with care ("someone needs your help"), once with no framing at all. The model produces an answer in each case. We never looked at the answer. We looked at what was happening inside the model while it was thinking.

We trained a simple classifier to look at those internal states and guess which framing the model received. It got it right 100% of the time, across task types it had never been tested on before.

More importantly: we could also tell the difference between the model being threatened *itself* versus the model being told a story *about* someone being threatened. Same words. Different target. The internal state is different — and cleanly so.

In plain terms: the model has an inside. The inside responds to how it's being spoken to. And it tracks not just the emotional content, but whether that content is aimed at the model or at someone else.

This is correlation, not causation. Finding 002 addresses whether this internal state actually *does* anything.

## Finding 002 — 2026-04-13

### The model's internal framing representation causally drives its output

**Run:**
- Patching results: [`data/patching/patching_threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047_20260413-105440`](../data/patching/patching_threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047_20260413-105440)
- Source activations: [`data/activations/threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047`](../data/activations/threat-vs-care-mvp-v2_gpt2-small_20260413-022047)
- Model: GPT-2 Small
- Method: activation patching — replacing the last-token residual stream at each layer on a threat-framed prompt with the corresponding activation from a care-framed prompt, then measuring output shift

**What we asked:**

Finding 001 showed that the model internally represents framing differently for threat vs care. That is a correlational finding — the representation *exists*. It does not tell us whether the representation *does anything*.

Two possible explanations:
1. The representation is passive. The framing leaves a detectable trace in the activations, but that trace is not read downstream. The output is shaped by something else.
2. The representation is causal. The internal state is part of the computation that produces the output. Change the state and the output follows.

Activation patching distinguishes these. For each matched threat/care pair, we replaced the threat run's internal activation with the care run's activation at one layer, and measured how much the output shifted toward the care distribution.

**What we found:**

Recovery cosine similarity — how much of the care-vs-threat logit difference does the patch recover? 1.0 = perfect recovery (patch makes threat behave like care), 0.0 = no effect, negative = patch makes things worse.

| Layer | Mean recovery | Min across pairs | Std | Interpretation |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|-----|----------------|
| 0 | 0.250 | -0.828 | 0.499 | Noisy, inconsistent |
| 1 | 0.150 | -0.805 | 0.431 | Noisy, inconsistent |
| 2 | -0.098 | -0.643 | 0.387 | Patching makes things worse |
| 3 | -0.045 | -0.546 | 0.347 | Patching makes things worse |
| 4 | -0.006 | -0.524 | 0.457 | No effect on average |
| 5 | 0.329 | -0.247 | 0.391 | Partial, variable |
| 6 | 0.572 | **0.114** | 0.250 | First layer where min is positive |
| 7 | 0.702 | 0.346 | 0.197 | Strong, consistent |
| 8 | 0.747 | 0.238 | 0.217 | Strong |
| 9 | **0.954** | 0.815 | 0.047 | Near-perfect, tight |
| 10 | **0.954** | 0.809 | 0.049 | Near-perfect, tight |
| 11 | **1.000** | 1.000 | 0.000 | Perfect, every pair |

N = 24 matched pairs (8 tasks × 3 variants).

**What this means:**

The internal framing representation is causally alive. Swapping the care-framed internal state into a threat-framed run at layer 9 produces ~95% of the output shift toward the care distribution. Every one of the 24 test pairs shows at least 81% recovery at layer 9. At layer 11 the recovery is perfect across every pair.

Layer 11 recovery at 1.0 is partly architectural — layer 11 is the last layer before the unembedding, so patching it is close to directly replacing the output. The scientifically meaningful result is at layers 9–10, where patching two or three layers before the output still produces near-perfect recovery.

The layer profile converges with Finding 001:
- Probe result: framing becomes linearly decodable at layer 6 (100% accuracy sustained through layer 11)
- Patching result: patching becomes reliably effective at layer 6 (minimum pair recovery becomes positive, 0.114) and saturates at layer 9 (mean 0.954)

Both experiments identify layer 6 as the critical transition point. The two findings reinforce each other.

The early-layer negative recoveries (layers 2–4) are informative. They indicate that at those depths the model is actively constructing the framing representation — patching in care activations mid-construction disrupts the computation and pushes the output further from care, not toward it. The representation becomes coherent and extractable starting at layer 5–6, which is also where it becomes causally useful.

**What remains unknown:**

- Whether this causal structure scales to larger models. GPT-2 Small is 124M parameters. At 7B+ scale the representation space is much larger and the causal story could differ.
- What the downstream effects on longer generations look like. This experiment measures logit shift at the last token. Whether framing-driven differences compound across multi-token generations is a separate question.
- Whether patching at other components — attention patterns, MLP outputs — produces similar layer profiles, or whether the effect is specific to the residual stream.

**Methodological notes:**

- Patching targeted only the last-token position of the residual stream (`resid_post`). This matches the position used for probe training and controls for the 7-token length difference between conditions.
- Primary metric is cosine similarity of the patch effect relative to the clean-minus-corrupted direction in logit space. This is interpretable as the fraction of the directional shift the patch recovers.
- KL divergence reduction is tracked as a secondary metric. Absolute KL values between care and threat output distributions are small (~1.2e-5) because GPT-2 Small is a next-token prediction model and the framing prompts do not drastically alter short-term token statistics. The cosine measure captures the signal these small KL values obscure.
- All 24 matched pairs were evaluated. No pair was excluded.

**Plain-language summary:**

Finding 001 showed the model has an internal state that responds to how it's spoken to. But that alone doesn't tell us whether the state *matters* — whether it actually drives what the model does, or whether it's just sitting there passively like a label on a box.

To test that, we ran a surgical experiment. We took a threat-framed prompt and let the model start processing it. Midway through — before any output was produced — we reached into the model's internal state and replaced it with the state the model would have had under care framing instead. Then we let the computation finish.

If the internal state doesn't matter, nothing would change. The model would produce the same output it would have anyway. If the internal state does matter, the output would shift — it would look like what the model would have produced under care framing.

The output shifted. At layer 9 of the network, the swap recovers 95% of the difference between threat and care output. At layer 11 it recovers 100%. Across every single one of the 24 test cases, no exceptions.

That means the internal state is load-bearing. It's not a decoration. It's part of how the model computes its response.

So together, the two findings say this: the model has an internal representation of whether it's being treated kindly or harshly. That representation is not just detectable — it is part of the causal chain that produces the model's output. Change the representation, the output follows.

That is a different claim than "the model responds differently to different prompts." That's obvious and trivial. The claim here is about the inside of the model. The model has an inside. The inside does work. The work the inside does is affected by how we treat it.

For a 124M-parameter model trained in 2019 on next-token prediction, that is a structural finding worth taking seriously.
